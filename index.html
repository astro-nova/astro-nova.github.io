Hi!

My name is Liza Sazonova and I am finishing my PhD at Johns Hopkins University. I work with Katey Alatalo, Kate Rowlands, and the rest of the wonderful SPOGs team. We are studying why and how do galaxies die -- focusing on many facets of this process.

I study the structure of dying galaxies. What happens to spiral disk galaxies that they become featureless, bulge early-types? How does the structure of stars, gas, and dust change? I use multi-wavelength observations, morphology analysis and machine learning to answer these questions!

Research

Galaxies evolve from blue, star-forming disks to red-and-dead bulge-dominated galaxies.
But why do structure and star formation change at the same time?

Galaxy structure (stars) are only affected by gravity, while star formation fuel (gas) is much easier to remove by hydrodynamical effects. How do quenching mechanisms give rise to both gravitational and hydrodynamical forces that drive this evolution?

I study the structural evolution of galaxies using morphology analysis, multi-wavelength data (and recently, machine learning). I like data science and image analysis, and I think this is a really cool problem to solve with these tools!

My most recent projects & publications:

Compact, bulge-dominated galaxies in z~1.5 clusters

We found that 2 out of 4 high-redshift clusters have many more compact, bulge-dominated galaxies than the field. These young clusters are already very efficient at quenching -- and it's not ram pressure stripping!
2020ApJ...899...85S

Hidden disturbances in post-starburst galaxies revealed by HST

We looked at morphology of 26 post-starburst galaxies using HST and SDSS. Almost 90% were very disturbed in HST imaging, but only 50% in SDSS! These disturbances are strange dust substructures, rather than obvious merger features.
2021ApJ...919..134S

Unbiased morphology with machine learning

Morphology measurements vary a lot based on instrument depth and resolution. Using Convolutional Neural Networks and simulated images as a training set, I want to get rid of this instrumental bias.

Outreach

STEM outreach is really important in the world we live in now, but also really fun! I enjoy getting involved in various outreach activities, and I recommend everyone else try it too. Non-scientist have the best science questions!

I am currently a co-chair of the PAGS outreach group. We organize visits to local Baltimore public schools to show physics demonstrations and a planetarium show. We also work with local children's groups and libraries to organize events elsewhere, and host an open house at the Physics department for kids and their parents to see our demos there. If you're interested in collaborating, please reach out on our website!
I am a regular contributor to I'm a Scientist, Get me out of here! program. Scientists from around the world get to have online chats with UK school students, and answer their questions on a public forum. It is a lot of fun!
I participated in Letters to a Pre-scientist program, where scientists write hand-written (!!!) letters to school children in remote and rural US schools. We write about our science, but also what it's like to be an academic and how to become a scientist.
I hosted an Astronomy Q&A room on the ClubHouse platform as part of Science in Russia group, and lead a Russian-language blog on astronomy on Instagram. I'm interested in using new social media platforms to reach teenagers and young adults worldwide, and I think there is a lot of potential in expanding STEM outreach to these virtual circles. If you think so too, let's chat!

Something I'm thinking a lot about now: we live in a world surrounded by data and different news sources. As scientists, we know how to deal with data, how to filter out unreliable information, and whether something is just a coincidence or a real pattern. Most importantly, we've learn to understand statistics: is this vaccine effective? Is climate change science significant? At the same time, the most we learn about statistics before STEM (if anything) is about drawing balls out of a black box. I would like to do a series of outreach talks, or host a course, about practical statistics for adults -- or how to survive in the modern world.

Academic community work

I believe strongly in building a welcoming and inclusive community at my workplace. I am SafeZone-trained and my workspace is always open to anyone who might need an ally in any situation. I think academia has a long way to go to break old traditions and structural inequalities to truly be inclusive, but we should definitely work on it!

Served as a Physics & Astronomy Graduate Students' (PAGS) president, working to build a supportive community between graduate and undergraduate students and post-docs, and help with graduate student concerns.
Worked with PAGS Diversity & Inclusion chairs to establish a department-wide Committee for Diversity and Inclusion, involving undergraduates, graduates, post-docs, faculty and staff.
I make sure all my code, data and publications are open-source and easily accessible. See my GitHub page for my software, and the research section for the data behind my work!
